Electronic health records (EHRs) are digital versions of patients' medical histories, diagnoses, treatments, prescriptions, and other health-related information. EHRs can improve the quality, safety, efficiency, and coordination of healthcare delivery, as well as empower patients to access and manage their own health data. However, creating an EHR system that is user-friendly for both patients and staff can be challenging, as it requires careful planning, design, implementation, and evaluation. In this article, we will share some tips on how to create an EHR system that is user-friendly for patients and staff.

1 Assess your needs

Before you start creating your EHR system, you need to assess your needs and goals. What are the main problems or gaps that you want to address with your EHR system? Who are your target users and what are their preferences, expectations, and challenges? How will your EHR system integrate with your existing workflows, processes, and systems? How will you measure the success and impact of your EHR system? By answering these questions, you can define the scope, objectives, and requirements of your EHR project.

2 Choose a suitable platform

Once you have assessed your needs, you need to choose a suitable platform for your EHR system. There are different types of EHR platforms available, such as cloud-based, server-based, or hybrid. Each platform has its own advantages and disadvantages, depending on your budget, security, scalability, and functionality needs. You also need to consider the compatibility and interoperability of your platform with other systems and devices that you or your users may use, such as smartphones, tablets, laptops, or scanners. You may want to consult with an expert or a vendor to help you select the best platform for your EHR system.

3 Design a user-friendly interface

Creating an EHR system that is user-friendly for patients and staff requires designing a user-friendly interface. This should be easy to navigate, understand, and use, as well as appealing and engaging. To achieve this, some best practices should be followed, such as using clear and consistent labels, icons, buttons, and menus; organizing information in a logical and intuitive way; minimizing the number of clicks, scrolls, and keystrokes required to perform a task; providing feedback and confirmation messages to the users; using appropriate colors, fonts, sizes, and contrasts to enhance readability and visibility; avoiding clutter, distractions, and unnecessary information; and incorporating user feedback and testing to improve the interface.

Most EHRs fail because they're built for checklists, not real workflows. Try this: Shadow your users for a full day. Note every extra click or manual step. Remove 3 of them in your next sprint. That's real usability. (Works for any software design).

4 Train and support your users

Creating an EHR system that is user-friendly for patients and staff requires training and supporting your users. This can help them adopt and use the system effectively and efficiently, while also helping to overcome any resistance or anxiety. To do this, you need to provide clear instructions and manuals on how to use the system, as well as offer different modes and formats of training, tailored to the different roles, levels, and needs of your users. Additionally, ongoing and timely support and troubleshooting should be provided through phone, email, chat, or help desk. Finally, you should encourage and reward your users for using the EHR system by providing incentives, recognition, or feedback.

5 Evaluate and improve your EHR system

Creating an EHR system that is user-friendly for patients and staff is an ongoing process that requires evaluation and improvement. To ensure the best performance, usability, and satisfaction of your EHR system, you should collect and analyze data and feedback from users through surveys, interviews, focus groups, or analytics. Comparing your results with goals, expectations, industry standards, and best practices can help you identify and prioritize areas that need improvement. After implementing and testing changes and enhancements with a pilot or prototype, it’s important to repeat the cycle of evaluation and improvement regularly and continuously. Doing so can improve the quality, safety, efficiency, and coordination of healthcare delivery while empowering patients to access and manage their own health data.
